{{ selected_origin_country_name }} Northern Ireland

If your product is only intended for use in the UK (including Northern Ireland) it will normally be subject to UK tariffs and charges. EU regulations apply to all goods in Northern Ireland.

If a proportion of the shipment is also intended to be used in the EU (such as Ireland), it may be considered ‘at risk’ and charged EU rates, partly depending on the amount or type of product. Some goods entering Northern Ireland may be automatically deemed ‘at risk’ and EU tariffs apply.

Some further information on how to determine whether your goods are ‘at risk’ or not can be found on our guidance page. This page also details how to declare your goods ‘not at risk’, if you consider them to be so.

If you need more information about which tariffs and charges apply to your shipment, you can contact:

  • your customs agent
  • your freight forwarder
  • your importer
  • the Trader Support Service

Your importer may be able to reclaim the difference between the EU and UK tariff if they can show that goods were only used within the UK.

The Trader Support Service is a useful source of information about trading into and out of Northern Ireland. You can visit their site for more detailed information about trading and Northern Ireland.

EU tariffs may apply where goods are exported to Northern Ireland for use in the EU (including Ireland), or are considered ‘at risk’ of being exported to the EU. These are shown in the table.

Meursing codes are used in the EU to apply variable specific duties to compound agri-goods (such as confectionary and bakery products), depending on the relative content of sugar, flour and milk starch in the product being imported.

You will need to work out the additional code using the Meursing code tool. To calculate the duty rate, enter the meursing code (without the 7 at the start) into the Meursing calculator to find variable duty elements such as AE, ADFM and ADSZ.
